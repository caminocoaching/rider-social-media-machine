## The Production Stack

The video production workflow uses three tools in sequence:

**Step 1: Claude (this project)** searches the web for the topic, finds a real article or study, writes the script, and specifies the slide deck content.

**Step 2: Manus** builds a slide deck with 5-8 clean slides covering the key points: the hook, the problem, the neuroscience, the racing application, and the CTA.

**Step 3: HeyGen** takes the slide deck as the background and overlays your AI avatar (cloned from your likeness) in a circle in the corner, narrating the script with your cloned ElevenLabs voice.

**Output:** A polished 30-90 second video that looks like you presenting over a professional slide deck. Believable, professional, and producible in under 15 minutes per video.

## Video Lengths by Platform

**Facebook Reels:** 30-60 seconds. Your data shows video posts on Facebook average 380 reach, but that's because you've been posting long-form videos. Short, punchy video with slides will perform differently because Facebook's algorithm currently favours Reels-length video content.

**Instagram Reels:** 15-60 seconds. Your IG data confirms shorter Reels outperform longer ones (9-second Reel got your highest IG reach at 499). Aim for 30-45 seconds as the sweet spot.

**Facebook native video (longer format):** 90 seconds to 3 minutes for deeper teaching content. Post as native video, not as a Reel. Use for the more complex neurochemistry explainers where you need more time.

## The Neurochemistry Layer

Every racing scenario has a chemical story happening in the rider's brain. This is your WOW factor. When you explain what cortisol does at the braking marker, or why dopamine makes qualifying feel different to race day, riders suddenly understand their experience through science rather than frustration.

Here is each chemical mapped to racing scenarios, content pillars, and video angles.

---

### CORTISOL (The Threat Chemical)

**What it does:** Released by the adrenal glands in response to perceived threat. Increases heart rate, narrows attention, triggers fight-or-flight. In small doses it sharpens focus. In sustained doses it destroys motor control, locks muscles, and kills flow state.

**Racing scenarios where cortisol dominates:**
- Standing on the grid before a race start
- Entering a corner where you crashed last time
- Seeing a rival closing in your mirrors
- Losing grip mid-corner and feeling the rear slide
- Rain starting during a dry setup session
- First lap of qualifying when the pressure is on

**Content pillar connections:** Race Pressure, Braking Zone Panic, Confidence, The 7 Mistakes (Mistake: trying too hard)

**Video angles:**
- "What your brain does in the 30 seconds before the lights go out" (grid anxiety)
- "Why you brake 5 metres earlier at the corner where you crashed" (trauma response)
- "Your rival is closing. Here's why your lap times just got worse, not better" (social threat)
- "Cortisol is the chemical that turns a fast rider into a cautious one. Here's when it fires."

**WOW not HOW boundary:** Reveal that cortisol floods the system and how it manifests (tight shoulders, early braking, choppy inputs). Never reveal the specific protocol for managing it (that's the programme).

**Slide deck structure (Manus):**
- Slide 1: Hook text (bold, dark background)
- Slide 2: The scenario (describe the on-track moment)
- Slide 3: "What's happening in your brain" with a simple brain diagram highlighting the amygdala
- Slide 4: The measurable cost (0.3-0.5s per corner, tighter lines, earlier braking)
- Slide 5: The bridge ("This is one of the 7 mental errors we measure in 2,249 debriefs")
- Slide 6: CTA slide (Rider Mindset Quiz or comment keyword)

---

### DOPAMINE (The Reward and Risk Chemical)

**What it does:** Released in anticipation of reward, not just after receiving it. Drives motivation, risk-taking, and novelty-seeking. Creates the sensation of "wanting" and pursuit. In flow state, dopamine is one of the five neurochemicals that produce the zone experience.

**Racing scenarios where dopamine dominates:**
- The buzz of qualifying well and wanting more
- Chasing a personal best and feeling the momentum build
- The excitement of a new track or a new bike
- Overtaking someone and wanting to overtake the next one immediately
- The addictive pull of "one more lap" when you should pit
- Pre-season excitement and goal-setting

**Content pillar connections:** Flow State, Confidence, Client Transformations, Tyre and Grip Management (why riders over-push when they feel fast)

**Video angles:**
- "The chemical reason you over-push after a fast lap" (dopamine spike drives risk)
- "Why your best laps happen when you're chasing, not defending" (dopamine is pursuit-based)
- "New track, new bike, instant speed. Here's the neurochemistry behind fresh motivation."
- "Dopamine made you fast in FP1. Then it disappeared in FP3. Here's why."

**WOW not HOW boundary:** Reveal that dopamine is anticipation-based and that it's one of the flow state chemicals. Explain why riders feel invincible after a fast lap and then crash on the next one. Never reveal the specific flow triggers or dopamine management techniques from the programme.

---

### SEROTONIN (The Confidence Chemical)

**What it does:** Regulates mood, social hierarchy perception, and feelings of significance. High serotonin = feeling calm, confident, and worthy of your grid position. Low serotonin = self-doubt, impostor syndrome, feeling like you don't belong.

**Racing scenarios where serotonin matters:**
- Impostor syndrome when stepping up to a faster class
- Feeling calm and "in your right" on the grid vs feeling like a fraud
- Post-win confidence that carries into the next round
- The spiral after a DNF or a bad weekend
- Comparing yourself to your teammate who's faster

**Content pillar connections:** Confidence, Race Pressure, Client Transformations (Cormac Buchanan impostor syndrome story)

**Video angles:**
- "Why Cormac Buchanan felt like he didn't belong in Moto3. And the chemical that changed." (serotonin and impostor syndrome)
- "The rider who's fast in testing but disappears in qualifying has a serotonin problem, not a speed problem."
- "Confidence isn't a personality trait. It's a chemical state. And it's trainable."
- "Why your best weekends start with one good session. The serotonin snowball effect."

**WOW not HOW boundary:** Reveal that confidence has a neurochemical basis and that it cascades (one good session builds serotonin for the next). Never reveal the Confidence Protocol or pre-session serotonin management techniques.

---

### OXYTOCIN (The Trust and Connection Chemical)

**What it does:** Released through social bonding, trust, and team connection. Creates feelings of safety and belonging. In racing context, it's the relationship between rider and team, rider and coach, rider and the bike itself. Low oxytocin = feeling isolated, unsupported, paranoid about team decisions.

**Racing scenarios where oxytocin matters:**
- Trust in your team's setup decisions
- The rider who performs better when their family is at the track
- The parent-child dynamic in junior racing
- Feeling connected to the bike vs fighting it
- The calm that comes from a pre-race routine shared with your team

**Content pillar connections:** Race Pressure, Sleep and Recovery, Client Transformations (Grant Payne team manager perspective)

**Video angles:**
- "Why some riders are faster when their family is trackside. It's not luck. It's oxytocin."
- "The chemical reason you ride better with a team you trust."
- "If you're fighting your bike, you might be fighting your brain's trust system."
- "Grant runs a race team in New Zealand. He noticed something change in his riders when they started this."

**WOW not HOW boundary:** Reveal the connection between trust/belonging and performance chemistry. Never reveal specific team-building or relationship-based protocols from the programme.

---

### TESTOSTERONE (The Dominance Chemical)

**What it does:** Drives competitive behaviour, aggression, risk tolerance, and territorial response. Peaks in competition. Rises after wins, drops after losses. Creates the "hunter" mindset on track.

**Racing scenarios where testosterone matters:**
- First lap aggression and race starts
- Defending position under attack
- The "red mist" that causes over-aggressive overtaking
- Post-win confidence and carrying momentum into the next race
- The psychological impact of losing: testosterone drops after defeats

**Content pillar connections:** Race Pressure, Confidence, The 7 Mistakes (over-push), MotoGP/WSBK Moments

**Video angles:**
- "Why your best race starts happen when you feel like a predator, not a prey animal."
- "The chemical reason you make risky overtakes you wouldn't attempt in practice."
- "Marc Marquez's brain chemistry after winning a race is measurably different from every other rider on the grid."
- "You lost Sunday's race. Here's what happened to your brain chemistry by Monday. And why it matters for next round."

**WOW not HOW boundary:** Reveal the competition/testosterone cycle and how wins and losses chemically affect subsequent performance. Never reveal recovery or testosterone-management techniques.

---

### ENDORPHINS (The Pain Gate Chemical)

**What it does:** Natural painkillers released during intense physical effort, stress, or after sustained exertion. Create the "high" feeling after a session. Mask physical discomfort. In motorsport, they reduce awareness of fatigue and physical strain during long sessions.

**Racing scenarios where endorphins matter:**
- Mid-race when arms pump and fatigue sets in but you stop noticing
- The euphoria after crossing the finish line on a good lap
- Why riders describe the best sessions as "effortless" even though they were physically demanding
- The addictive nature of track days and racing
- Recovery: why you feel exhausted hours after a session but felt fine at the time

**Content pillar connections:** Flow State, Tyre and Grip Management (sustained sessions), Sleep and Recovery

**Video angles:**
- "Why you feel nothing during a flow lap but everything hurts an hour later."
- "The chemical reason some sessions feel effortless and others feel like a fight."
- "Endorphins are your brain's natural painkiller. Here's when they switch on during a race."
- "The post-session crash is real. Here's the neurochemistry behind it."

**WOW not HOW boundary:** Reveal the endorphin cycle and its relationship to flow state. Never reveal specific endorphin-triggering techniques or recovery protocols.

## The Chemical Stack in Flow State

This is the most powerful video concept available to you. Flow state is not one chemical. It is a cocktail of all six working together. This is the WOW that no other motorsport content creator is teaching.

**The Flow Cocktail:**
- Dopamine (focus and pursuit drive)
- Norepinephrine (arousal and alertness)
- Endorphins (pain masking and effort reduction)
- Serotonin (confidence and calm)
- Anandamide (pattern recognition and lateral thinking)

**Video angle:** "Flow state is not a mindset. It is a measurable chemical cocktail in your brain. Five neurochemicals firing in sequence. When they align, you process at 4 billion bits per second. When they don't, you're stuck at 110. Here's what triggers each one."

**WOW not HOW:** Name the chemicals. Explain what each one does in the context of a fast lap. Show that flow is neurobiological, not mystical. Never reveal the specific sequence of triggers or the training protocol that produces the cocktail on demand.

## Video Script Template (For the AI to Generate)

Every video script the AI produces should follow this structure:

**HOOK (0-5 seconds)**
One sentence. Text overlay on the first slide. Must stop the scroll.
"Your brain releases cortisol 0.3 seconds before you reach the braking marker. Here's what it does to your riding."

**SCENARIO (5-15 seconds)**
Describe the on-track moment the rider recognises.
"You're approaching Turn 3. You crashed here two rounds ago. Your hands tighten on the bars. Your braking point moves 5 metres earlier. You didn't decide to brake early. Your brain decided for you."

**THE SCIENCE (15-35 seconds)**
Name the chemical. Explain what it does in plain language. One slide with a simple visual.
"That's cortisol. Your brain's threat detection system flooding your body with stress hormones because it remembers this corner is dangerous. It narrows your vision, tenses your muscles, and switches you from automatic riding to conscious control."

**THE COST (35-45 seconds)**
Quantify the impact in lap time or race position.
"In our data from 2,249 debriefs, riders with elevated stress markers in specific corners consistently lose 0.3 to 0.5 seconds per corner compared to their own baseline. That's not a bike problem. That's a brain problem."

**THE BRIDGE (45-55 seconds)**
Connect to the bigger picture. Tease the solution without giving it away.
"The fix is not more bravery. Trying harder makes cortisol worse. There's a specific process for retraining your brain's response to that corner. That's what we work on."

**CTA (55-60 seconds)**
Casual, low-pressure.
"If you want to find out which mental patterns are costing you the most, I built a free Rider Mindset Quiz. 12 real racing scenarios. 3 minutes. Link in the comments."

## Manus Slide Deck Specifications

Each video needs a 5-8 slide deck from Manus. Here are the design specs the AI should pass to Manus:

**Overall style:** Dark background (#0A1628 or #000000). Clean sans-serif font (Montserrat or similar). Teal accent (#00BFA5) for key data points. Minimal text per slide. Maximum 15 words per slide.

**Slide 1 (Hook):** Bold text only. The hook sentence. Large font. No images. This is the scroll-stopper frame.

**Slide 2 (Scenario):** Short scenario description. Could include a simple track diagram or corner illustration.

**Slide 3 (The Chemical):** Name of the chemical in large teal text. One-line description of what it does. Optional: simple brain region highlight or chemical structure icon (keep it clean, not clinical).

**Slide 4 (The Mechanism):** How the chemical manifests in riding. 2-3 bullet points maximum. Example: "Tighter grip. Earlier braking. Narrowed vision."

**Slide 5 (The Data):** One stat from the debrief data. Large number. Short label. Source line. This is the Data Visualisation Card format from the Visual Content System.

**Slide 6 (The Bridge):** "This is one of the patterns we measure across 2,249 debriefs." Or a teaser line about the solution.

**Slide 7 (CTA):** "Free Rider Mindset Quiz" with the URL or comment keyword. Clean, simple.

**Slide 8 (Optional - End Card):** Camino Coaching logo. "84 five-star reviews on Trustpilot."

## HeyGen Production Notes

**Avatar position:** Bottom-right corner circle. Small enough not to obscure the slide content but large enough that facial expressions are visible.

**Avatar behaviour:** Talking directly to camera. Natural gestures. Not reading from a script (HeyGen should render conversational delivery, not teleprompter style).

**Voice:** ElevenLabs clone of Craig's voice. Conversational pace. UK English. Remember: ElevenLabs requires numbers written out in full text (e.g., "two thousand two hundred and forty nine" not "2,249").

**Transitions:** Simple slide transitions. No flashy effects. The content and the avatar carry the video, not the production.

**Output format:** Vertical (9:16) for Reels. Horizontal (16:9) for longer Facebook native video.
